Part 2: Medicine programme in-depth

July 7, 2017 in UBD Medicine

This is part 2 of the post regarding the medicine programme that is available in Universiti Brunei Darusalam. All these information are correct. They are based on author experience and are also gathered from the main website.

This post will be updated if the contents are outdated. Please do contact the author if there are any mistake or problem in the post.

If you haven’t read Part 1 regarding the Medicine Programme, click here. Unlike other courses in UBD except for Chemical & Process engineering, Medicine has a fixed compulsory modules(subjects). Therefore, you don’t have to worry about the timetable/exam clashes. Unfortunately, you can’t take other non-compulsory breath modules (i.e Languages, Economics and etc). Your schedule is already tight and taking other breath modules is insane! This is what the timetable looks like.

Tl;dr

  • Busy life
  • Many things to study
  • You get to talk with real patients
  • Exam is fucking tough
  • Medicine is not for everyone

Medicine Modules:

  • Health Sciences (HS)
  • Our Community and Professional Personal Developement (OC/PPD)
  • Special Study Module (SSM)
  • Patient Care (PC)
  • Clinical Placement (3rd year only)

Compulsory breath modules:

  • Melayu Islam Beraja
  • Islamic Civilisation (Taught in english)
  • Communication Skills I & II (aka English language)

Health Sciences

  • Basic Sciences
  • Clinical Sciences

PBL is part of the Health Sciences module, as the LOBS that you’ve learned will definitely come out in exam. Health Sciences is the toughest exam when compared to other Medicine modules. There are so many things to learn (14 cases/systems in 1 semester or 14 weeks), so time management and dedication to studying is important! People drop out of the course because of this module.

OC/PPD

  • Ethics, Law, Teamwork, Islamic Medicine, Audit
  • Biostatistics, Epidemiology, Research methods, Evidence Based Medicine

OC as the name implies, is all about Brunei community health and what health services that are available to tackle these community problems.

PPD, is where you get to learn all the medical laws, ethics, biostatistics and islamic medicine. An example, can a diabetic patient fast?(Yes we actually use Dr.Omar Kasule Notes).

Patient Care

This module teaches your practical & communication skills with patients on how to take a history (What’s their complain?), examinations (Neurological, Abdominal, Cardiovascular and more) and also procedures (stitching, Urethral catherisation). This skills are very important, as you will apply all those skills during your clinical visit/attachment in RIPAS which is conducted once a week for 1st & 2nd years and twice a week for 3rd years.

Special Study Module

For each year, the SSM module will be different.

1st year (SSM1), follow up a pregnant woman until delivery and write a full report (5 sections = 5000 words)

2nd year (SSM2), do any research; requires ethical approval from both IHS Research Ethics Committee & Ministry of Health Research Ethics Committe to actually conduct the research. You even get to publish or even present in a symposium if your research is really good.

3rd year (SSM3), conduct a community programme; An example, L.I.F.T.I.N.

Clinical Placement

This module is only for 3rd years and you are required to do at least 40 clinical skills in the hospital, record a 10 minute video of you interviewing a real patient and write a total of 6 reflective portfolio.

Clinical skills including doing vene-puncture on real patients (Taking blood!), Male Urethral catherisation (Insert tubes into their dick!) and sponge-bathing a patient (Whew! Respect to all the staffs doing this everyday!).

Yes, you need to record a video of you taking a history from a real patient so you need to find patients in the wards and ask for permission! Some patient are talktative so you need control them >.< due to time limitation.

Reflective portfolio is basically writing at least 100 words on the problem you’ve encountered during your clinical visits/attachment and what you can learn from them. The porfolio are written based on a list of topic given to you in the beginning of year, i.e Ethical practise and legal responsibilities and Memorable patients.

How’s Medicine taught in Brunei?

Medicine in Brunei are taught with Problem Based Learning + Lecture, like most UK medical schools. In the 1st year also known as foundation year, all 5 available courses in IHS are taught together which includes both lectures and PBL.

In the 2nd & 3rd year, PBL is only held for medical students. By this time, you are already expert in PBLing.

How’s Problem Based Learning conducted?

Basically, you will be given a patient’s case and you have to discuss with your mates about the patient’s problem. As you discuss, you might come across unknown words or even wtf is wrong with the patient. All these questions goes under Learning Objectives(LOBS) for you to learn (either through lectures, books or internet) and present in the next PBL session.

From PBL, you are going to learn all your basic sciences intensively. For an example, you learn Gastrointestinal system (including related GI diseases) within approximately 2 weeks, which is fucking insane! Each week, the theme of the case will be different. Below is just a rough example.

Week Case System
1 Colorectal Cancer Oncology
2 Respiratory distress of infancy Paediatrics
3 Depression Psychiatric

Basic Sciences (NOT FOR THE FAINT OF HEART!!)

  • Anatomy & Physiology
  • Microbiology
  • Biochemistry
  • Pharmacology
  • Clinical diseases

In most people’s opinion, PBL is an efficient method to study when compared to lectures only. Teaching others what you know is a really good way to judge your own knowledge. Personally, i prefer Anki (Spaced-memory repetition is the best) and sometimes i skipped lectures.

“I hear and I forget.”

“I see and I remember.”

“I do and I understand.”

What about the exams??

There are 2 papers and 1 Objective Structured Clinical Examination (OSCE).

Health Science paper

  • 100 Multiple Choice Questions (MCQ) and 5 Short Answer Questions (SAQ) for 3 hours.

EVERTHING CUM OUT! ‘Nuff said

OC/PPD paper

  • 20 SAQ for 3 hours.

Memorise the shit out of all the slides and notes given to you and KONPOM 99.9% A*!

OSCE

  • 10 stations; 1 station = 7 minutes.

DON’T BE AN ASSHOLE. There are 30 of you so BE POLITE TO THE SIMULATED PATIENTS (No real patients or is it?).

PRACTISE ALL THOSE SKILLS WITH YOUR FRIENDS. They are graded based on the average performance of your course-mate.

What if i failed the exams??

Unfortunately, it is quite common for people to fail Medicine including who have CRAZYSMARTASS A-LEVEL GRADES. The passing mark is 60% (Grade C). Trying to achieve a pass is already a great achievement! You can only do supplementary exam once which will be 3 weeks (i think?) after results are out.

Year 1

  • Dropped out of the programme if failed supplementary exam
  • Repetition of module is not allowed
  • Change to another programme

Year 2 & 3

  • Have a chance to repeat failed module and continue to 4th year after failing supplementary exam
  • Repeated students are now allowed to proceed to PMS
  • Change to another programme

Basically, when you’ve failed the supplementary exam in the 2nd or 3rd year, you will not be able to get your Medical Degree. In other words, you cannot be a medical doctor. In this case, you will continue to 4th year and do another research (Same as SSM2) to complete your study (BHSc Medicine, but no medical license).

Extra-curriculum events

There are a handful of event that you should join and volunteer. They are good for your experience!

  • International Summer Medical School (Kagawa University, Japan)
  • International Winter Medical School (Kagawa University, Japan)
  • Universitas Malaysia Sabah Anatomy Workshop
  • Malaysia-Indonesia-Brunei Medical Conference
  • Regional Inter-university Physiology Quiz Competition
  • University or Faculty Student Council
  • Health checks
  • Blood donation drives and other charity drives
  • Sport events
